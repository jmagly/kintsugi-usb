# About the Name

## Kintsugi — 金継ぎ

**Kintsugi** (*kin-TSOO-ghee*) is the Japanese art of repairing broken pottery with lacquer mixed with powdered gold. The repair is deliberately visible. The cracks are highlighted rather than hidden, and the mended object is considered more valuable than it was when whole.

## Etymology

The word is a compound of two elements:

| Character | Reading | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| 金 | *kin* | gold (borrowed from Middle Chinese) |
| 継ぎ | *tsugi* | to patch, join, mend; an act of joining |

Literally: **"golden joinery"** or **"golden repair."** A closely related term, *kintsukuroi* (金繕い, "golden mending"), predates *kintsugi* in some sources and carries the same meaning.

## Origin

The most widely-told origin story places the technique in the late 15th century. The shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa broke a favorite Chinese tea bowl and sent it back to China to be repaired. It returned mended with unsightly metal staples. Dissatisfied, Yoshimasa commissioned Japanese craftsmen to develop a more beautiful method of repair. They began filling the cracks with *urushi* lacquer dusted or mixed with powdered gold, silver, or platinum.

The practice became closely tied to *chanoyu* (Japanese tea ceremony) ceramics. By the Edo period (17th century) it had evolved into a recognized craft and aesthetic tradition.

## Philosophy

Kintsugi is philosophically adjacent to *wabi-sabi* — the embrace of impermanence, imperfection, and the beauty of things worn or incomplete. Its central idea is that **breakage and repair are part of an object's history**, not something to disguise. A kintsugi-repaired bowl is not a compromised version of the original; it is a richer one. The gold seams are its biography.

## Why it fits this project

A rescue USB lives at the exact point where systems break. Its job is not to prevent failure — it is to be there when failure has already happened, and to make the repair *better* than the silent assumption that nothing was ever wrong.

- **A fleet host breaks.** The USB boots it.
- **You don't know what's wrong.** The USB carries AI — online via `claude`, offline via `llama.cpp` — that can read runbooks, diagnose, and guide repair.
- **The repair itself becomes part of the system's story.** Host-specific AGENT-CONTEXT packs record the break and the recovery path, so the next incident starts with more knowledge than the last.
- **The gold is the AI and the accumulated fleet knowledge** carried on the drive. It fills the cracks that generic rescue media leave bare.

The metaphor is literal, not decorative. Most rescue distros aim to restore a system to the state it was in before the break, as if the break never happened. Kintsugi USB takes the opposite stance: the break is data, the recovery is a seam, and the fleet is stronger for having the record.

## Pronunciation

- **Kintsugi**: *kin-TSOO-ghee* (three syllables; the middle "tsu" is a single soft sound, not "tuh-soo")
- The *u* in *tsu* is nearly silent in native speech, so it often sounds closer to *kin-TSGHEE* at speed.
